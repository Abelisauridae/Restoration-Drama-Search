DEM.

Tryphon, I grant, through Seas of blood has gone
To force his passage to the Syrian Throne;
But how men gain their Pow'r the Gods do not
So much regard, as how 'tis us'd when got.
Our Murther'd King Antiochus did own,
That Tryphons Father did restore his Crown;
Which made him trust the Sons ambitious youth
With so much pow'r as did corrupt his truth:
This our dead Prince discover'd, but too late,
Which did provoke Tryphon to act his fate:
For Kings should not to too great Subjects shew
They mean their ruine, till they act it too;
And he too much deserves to lose his Throne
Who makes a Subject's power exceed his own.
What ever you in his defence have brought,
Rather than lessen doth augment his fault;
For what could show this Tyrant more unjust
Than to abuse such gratitude and trust;
Trust, which the wicked often does reclame,
This Monster's wickedness does more inflame.
That he repents, nothing can us perswade,
Since what makes others good has made him bad.
When once Ambition does the mind devour,
Men Sacrifice their Vertue to their Pow'r.
Antiochus had rais'd him up so high,
As he was either to usurp, or die;
And when he had perceiv'd the Kings distrust,
It made him think, that what was safe was just.
Oh let him not be pleaded for by you,
Who did his King depose and murther too,
Lest on your self th' Usurpers guilt you bring;
'Tis less to kill, than to arraign a King;
And he who does an Empires loss endure
Cannot think death a punishment, but cure.

But Actions should be taken as they're meant.
TO vindicate him is not my intent;
Since all which can in his excuse be said
Is, that his Pride his Virtue has betray'd.
NO glimps of vertue e're in him could shine
Who kill'd his King, and all the Royal line.
After he had the chief of it supprest,
He thought it was unsafe to spare the rest.
Ambition made him act the Parricide,
And Cruelty must then preserve his Pride.
B y the same rule he ought to shed the blood
Of all his Country-men that dare be good:
Then from the Throne let us th' Usurper fling,
To save our selves, and to Revenge the King:
For should we to this Tyranny submit,
We shall deserve as well as suffer it:
And to the virtuous 'twill much worse appear
Such a misfortune to deserve than bear.
That Tryphon calls you favourite I know,
But to his Fear alone that name you ow:
Y e t though the kindness which he feigns were true,
It layes the ground of what I call you to ;
From you he merits death, since he hath dar'd
To hope such Friendship could such Guilt have spar'd.
Whom Tryphon fears he doth to death pursue,
And if he fear'd me, he might kill me too,
But, that he fears me not, he could not give
A stronger proof than that he lets me live.
Yet do not think his friendship such a charm,
As from revenge it could confine my arme;
But if my patience you a sin esteem,
It springs from Love to Syria, not to him :
For since the Royal line are made away,
Were Tryphon kill'd, who should the Scepter sway?
All the Ambitious for the Throne would fight,
For where none has the Title, all have Right.
Thus while we cast a bloody Tyrant down,
B y Blood we raise another to the Crown.
'Tis this, 'tis this which chiefly frightens me,
We may change Tyrants, not the Tyranny.
Where Force is Title, Force must make it good,
And who comes in by Armes must reign by Blood.
Banish such groundless fears, for he alone
Who kills the Tyrant should ascend his Throne.
Who from this Tyranny does Syria free,
All will confess, deserves our King to be.

EDITOR'S

PREFACE

S O U R C E S . Langbaine in his Account of the English Dramatick
Poets reports that Orrery took his plot for Guzman from the Spanish
romance of the same name. If Langbaine had ever taken the trouble
to verify the accuracy of what is patently no more than a surmise
on his part, he would have discovered that Mateo Aleman's picaresque novel, Guzman de Alfarache, contains neither characters nor
situations even slightly resembling those in Orrery's play. A. W.
Ward in his History of English Dramatic Literature (ed. 1899, hi,
345) is content to state simply that the comedy "points to some
Spanish source," while Allardyce Nicoli in his History of Restoration
Drama (p. 245) speaks more positively and says Guzman is "borrowed from a Spanish source." Extensive search of Spanish tales
and drama, however, and consultation with several leading authorities on Spanish literature have failed to yield any definite source.
And the more minutely the fabric of Orrery's plot is studied, the
more probable it seems that he invented the whole flimsy structure
of his rather dull story.
Yet Guzman differs markedly from the then prevailing patterns
of Restoration comedy. No farce so elaborately developed out of
both word and action had hitherto arisen among the new creations
of the age. Orrery's mixture of verbal and physical buffoonery with
a gay, romantic intrigue possessed a flavor and an effect quite at
variance with the conventional modes. His novel design evidently
grew from observation that not only the Spanish comedy of intrigue had become a highly popular dramatic form at the moment,
but also that the commedia dell' arte, as interpreted by either Italian
or French performers in London, was extravagantly tickling the
fancy of audiences. In the Prologue to Tarugo1 s Wiles, acted in 1667
at the Duke's Theatre, the "Poet's Servant" remarks that " a
Trivolino, or a Skaramuchio that's dextrous at making of mouths
will sooner raise a Clap than a high flown fancy." To the same
effect are the words of Edward Howard in the Epistle prefixed in
1668 to the text of his play, The Usurper:

The other extream [of bad taste] which deserves some reflection; and which
far more debases the Dignity of the Stage, is that of Farce or Scommatick Plays,

which so tickled some late Audiences, with I know not what kind of Jollity, that
true Comedy is fool'd out of Countenance, and instead of Humour and wit, . . .
leaves it to the inheritance of Changlings.

This rage for Italian, or frequently Franco-Italian, farce presumably suggested the absurd antics exhibited by certain persons in
Guzman, notably Guzman, Oviedo, and Piracco. Moreover, these
characters, as well as Francisco, Guivarro, and Alvares, gay, clever
blades who must exist by their wits and tricks, indubitably reflect
the influence of the conventional commedia dell' arte personages.
The title part especially reveals this influence, for Guzman's traits
distinctly recall the Pantaloon and the Captain of contemporary
Italian tradition.
Orrery's indebtedness, however, to foreign models must not be
overemphasized. English comic material of his day includes characterizations not greatly unlike some to be found in Guzman. For
example, the "old, covetous, rich, amorous, cowardly buffoon," as
Guzman himself is described in the Dramatis Personae, who dotes
on grotesquely bombastic language and continually struts with pretended bravado, is a close counterpart to a Shirley character seen
again on the London stage about this time. In Shirley's comedy,
Love Tricks, or The School of Compliments, appears prominently a
rich gull named Bubulcus, who, like Guzman, talks continual fustian,
puts on brave airs, and tries to woo for himself a fair young lady
by spouting foolish love verses. This comedy had been revived at
the Duke's Theatre with so great success in 1667 that a new edition
of the play was published during that same year. Since Love Tricks
was presumably still on the stage in 1668 (for Pepys saw it on January 7), Orrery may have been present at a performance after his
return to London in June. At all events, its fresh popularity must
have called his attention to the success of such farcical characters
as Bubulcus, whose behavior is closely akin to Italian comic manners.
The pseudo-Spanish atmosphere in which Orrery cloaks the action
of Guzman follows the already established vogue of intrigue comedy
set in a Spanish scene and performed by characters of Spanish name.
Tuke's The Adventures of Five Hours (L. I. F. 1663), and Dryden's
The Wild Gallant (T. R. 1663), the pioneer Restoration creations,
initiated the renewal of this Jacobean and Caroline fashion. Orrery,
however, chooses Salamanca, a most unusual scene even in Spanish
comedy. The reason for his choice is not far to seek. He utilizes
as an important plot device the disguise of Francisco in the role of
an astrologer. Now Salamanca from the Middle Ages onwards had
been the particular rendezvous of scholars interested in astrology
